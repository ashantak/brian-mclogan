The emotional layer

The system reads the room so you can show up for the students who need it most

Comment intelligence scans for three emotional signals beyond questions and student wins.

Signal 1: Frustration
"I still don't get this," "I've watched this 10 times," "nothing is working." These students are still showing up. They have not quit. They need acknowledgment, not another explanation of the same concept. The system flags these and drafts a reply that acknowledges the difficulty and points to a different entry point.

"This finally clicked after two years," "passed my final because of you," "my teacher couldn't explain it but you did." These are the moments that remind you why you built this. The system flags them and drafts a personal reply. It also surfaces them to you separately so you can write the ones you want to write yourself.

Signal 3: Persistence
"Failed three times and still trying," "I know I'm probably not your typical student but," "this is my last chance to pass." These are the students who need to hear from Brian specifically. The system flags these as high priority and marks them for a personal reply. No draft. Just a flag. Because some things should not be automated.

Where these numbers come from: 2026 sources

The rate ranges below are pulled from three sources: SponsorRadar's 2026 sponsorship rate database (which tracks active brand campaigns across YouTube), Influesque's 2026 creator rate guide (which uses $15–25 CPM as the standard band for Tier-1 market integrations), and Vivian Agency's 2026 influencer rate report (which places mega-tier creators at 1M+ subscribers in the $20K+ per integration range).

One thing to keep in mind: subscriber count puts you in a tier, but average views per video is what determines the actual rate. Brands pay based on how many people will see the sponsorship, not how many subscribed. A channel averaging 300,000 views per video lands very differently than one averaging 15,000 views, even at the same subscriber count. The rate card gets set after looking at your real numbers together, so the figures here are the benchmark range for your tier. Your actual number could be higher or lower.

Frequently asked questions

Some things
worth talking through.

Straightforward answers to what you are probably thinking.

What does this actually do for me as a creator who got into this to help students?▾
Most of the time you spend after recording right now goes into things that have nothing to do with teaching. Descriptions, thumbnails, emails, distribution. None of that is why you built this. The system takes those tasks off your plate so your time goes back to the part that matters: making content that helps students understand math.

The second thing it does is let the student relationship scale in a way it cannot right now. You cannot personally read every comment or follow up with every student who is struggling. The system makes sure the ones who need to hear from you get flagged, the ones who had a breakthrough get acknowledged, and students get content that follows their learning path automatically. You have built 15 years of trust with 1.62 million people. This just makes sure that trust keeps compounding instead of getting buried under the operational side of things.
When I pick a video idea, what do I get to record from?▾
When you approve a video idea from the dashboard, the system generates a pre-recording brief before you record anything. It includes the topic statement, problems in difficulty progression order, specific mistakes to address based on comment data and watch time drop-off, and prerequisite concept reminders. The structure follows your existing lesson format, learned from your transcript history. Not a generic outline.

The worksheet problems are pre-selected in the same brief, so recording and resource creation come from the same document. Review it in five minutes, adjust anything you want, and record. If you want to teach it differently, you can. The brief is a starting point, not a script.
What does this cost, and are there ongoing costs beyond the retainer?▾
There is a one-time build fee and a monthly retainer to manage and maintain the system. The exact figures depend on which phases you want and what your current setup looks like.

Beyond the retainer, there are infrastructure costs that run in the background. These are yours to own: AI generation costs via API (Claude or equivalent) typically run $80–$120 per month at your upload volume. Transcription via AssemblyAI runs roughly $15–$30 per month. A workflow automation platform like Make runs $20–$50 per month. Dashboard hosting is minimal. Total infrastructure outside the retainer: roughly $150–$250 per month depending on your editing choice and tools kept.

On tools: the system can replace Super Agent entirely once running. VidIQ is worth keeping at a basic plan level for its YouTube trend data, which the system does not replicate fully. You could drop it, but $16 per month for the data it provides is reasonable to keep.
What about video editing?▾
The system does not replace editing. You have three options.

Option 1: Descript. Built specifically for talking-head and educational formats. You edit by deleting text from the transcript. Filler words, bad takes, and silence removed automatically. For your format, this is genuinely practical. Cost: $24–$36 per month. Vizard and Zapier both rate it the best transcript-first editor for educational content in their 2026 reviews.

Option 2: Part-time editor. For your format, typically $15–$30 per video. Around $200–$400 per month at 3 uploads per week. Their only job is the cut. The system handles everything after the file is dropped.

Option 3: Continue as-is. The system still handles everything after editing is done. Editing time is separate and is not included in any time savings calculation.
Can I customize or expand it as I go?▾
Yes, and that is actually how it is designed to work. The system gets more accurate over time as it accumulates data about what performs on your specific channel. Thumbnail styles, email timing, content recommendations, brief structure: all of it improves as the system learns your audience.

Beyond that, the system has a built-in suggestion layer. As it runs, it identifies patterns and surfaces recommendations for what should be added or changed next: a new automation that could save additional time, a distribution channel that is underperforming, a segment of your audience that is not being served well. You review those suggestions and decide what to act on. The system does not change itself without your approval. But it does tell you what it thinks should come next, and that gets sharper the longer it runs.
Where do the sponsorship rate numbers come from?▾
Three sources, all from 2025–2026. SponsorRadar's active campaign database places the standard integration CPM band for Tier-1 markets at $15–25 per thousand views. Influesque's 2026 creator rate guide applies the same band by channel tier. Vivian Agency's 2026 influencer rate report places channels over 1 million subscribers at $20,000 or more per integration as a baseline.

The caveat: subscriber count puts you in a tier. Average views per video determines the actual rate. Brands pay based on how many people will see the sponsorship, not how many subscribed. The rate card for your channel gets set after confirming your actual average view count. The figures in this document are the benchmark range for your tier. Your real number may be higher or lower.

What do I actually have to do during the build?▾
Three setup conversations before anything is built. First: channel audit, workflow review, priorities confirmed. Second: voice and format calibration, going through examples of your best-performing content so the system learns your tone and structure. Third: API access and connections for YouTube and Kajabi, both straightforward and reversible at any time.

After that, you approve the first several uploads through the system as it runs. That approval window is where adjustments get made based on what you actually want. Once calibrated, your daily job is reviewing three items in the dashboard after each video is ready and tapping approve.
What if I want to stop?▾
The system lives in your accounts. YouTube API connections are to your channel. Kajabi automations are in your Kajabi account. Email sequences are yours. If you stop working together, everything built keeps running in your accounts. You own what was built, not access to someone else's platform.
